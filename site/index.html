CAMMELOT

I built a town to stress-test whether AI can save Dutch healthcare.
Then I ran it 200 times.

45 citizens · 3 GPs · 1 hospital · 200 simulation runs · Real Dutch data

Why I Built This

I work at Microsoft. You should assume I’m biased toward AI. That’s exactly why I built something that could prove me wrong. In several important ways, it did.

Both online and offline, I still see the AI conversation stuck in a strange place. Proponents treat it as a productivity tool — a faster way to write emails. Critics dismiss it as hype. Neither side shows their math. “AI will solve the zorginfarct.” How? Which interventions? In what order? With what side effects? “AI is overhyped.” Compared to what? The system that’s already producing a 301,000-worker shortage by 2035?

I wanted a machine that produces numbers. Not predictions — outputs you can measure. Run it, measure it, break it, run it again. Two hundred times. With statistics.

So I built Cammelot: a simulated Dutch town. Forty-five citizens with names, ages, pre-existing conditions, and GP assignments. Three general practitioners. One hospital. The citizens walk around a 16-bit pixel map that looks like it belongs on a 1994 Super Nintendo — but their diseases are drawn from 2025 RIVM prevalence data, their healthcare costs from NZa tariffs, and their waiting times from the Treeknorm, the Dutch legal norm for specialist care access.

The Town of Cammelot

The intellectual lineage runs through Park et al.’s “Generative Agents: Interactive Simulacra of Human Behavior” (Stanford, 2023) — the paper that put 25 AI agents in a town called Smallville and watched them organize a Valentine’s Day party. My agents don’t organize parties. They enter waiting lists. They develop COPD. They wait twelve weeks for a cardiologist and sometimes they don’t make it.

Variables, Not Vibes

Every claim in this project is backed by simulation data, statistical tests, and published source parameters. The code is open. Tell me where I’m wrong.

The Crisis: IST

IST is German for “current state.” In Cammelot, the IST world is today’s Dutch healthcare system, parameterized from CBS, NZa, LHV, and IZA data. It is the control group. Nothing is hypothetical — every number traces to a published source.

r GP is operating at sixty-two percent capacity before they see their first patient. The effective capacity formula: C_eff = C_total × (1 − 0.30 − 0.08) × 1.0 = 62%. Thirty percent lost to paperwork. Eight percent to sick leave. No AI assistance. The efficiency multiplier is 1.0.

When citizens get sick in IST, they follow the same path real Dutch patients do: visit a GP, get triaged, get referred to a specialist, and wait. If the wait exceeds the Treeknorm threshold, their health deteriorates. The sicker you already are, the faster you decline. At some point, the sprite turns grey. A ghost. A preventable death caused not by the disease itself, but by the system’s inability to process the patient in time.

How the Disease Engine Works

Every citizen’s health trajectory is governed by Markov State Machines — probabilistic models calibrated to RIVM 2025 prevalence data. A patient with COPD transitions between states: healthy → mild → moderate → severe → critical. Each simulation tick, the chain rolls. There is no script. There is no predetermined outcome. The dice are loaded with real epidemiological data, and the results are stochastic.

Comorbidities compound the odds. Diabetes worsens cardiovascular disease (relative risk 2.0×). COPD accelerates heart failure (2.5×). Hypertension compounds both (1.8×). These aren’t arbitrary multipliers — they come from RIVM comorbidity research. A 72-year-old with diabetes and COPD isn’t just sicker. They’re on a steeper curve, and a 12-week wait hits them fundamentally differently than it hits a 35-year-old with a sports injury.

The Disease Engine

Markov chains calibrated to RIVM prevalence data (2025) for COPD, Diabetes Type 2, Cardiovascular disease, Dementia, and Arthrosis. Each citizen’s trajectory is probabilistic, not scripted.

The Regulatory Framework

Patient flow follows NZa tariffs and Treeknormen — the legal maximum waiting times for specialist care. GP consults cost €12.43. A prevented hospitalization saves €5,845. These are real numbers.

HP Drain: The Mortality Mechanism

Every citizen has a health bar. When wait times exceed the Treeknorm, HP drains at a rate determined by severity, comorbidities, and administrative burden. When it hits zero, the citizen becomes a ghost. This is the model’s core claim: system delay kills.

How It Actually Works

Every citizen in Cammelot is an autonomous agent running a cognitive loop. Each agent, every simulation tick, does three things:

1. Memory

Every action — GP visits, referrals, queue entries, diagnoses — is logged as an HL7® FHIR® R4 resource. The international healthcare data standard. Not a toy format; the actual standard that hospitals use. Each agent’s “life history” is a chronological FHIR record: Conditions, Encounters, Observations, MedicationRequests.

2. Reflection

Agents assess their own state. A patient “realizes” their breathing is getting worse. A GP notices their burnout climbing. These reflections drive behavior — when to seek care, when to refer, when to escalate. This is the Park et al. architecture applied to healthcare instead of a social village.

3. Planning

Based on memory and reflection, agents act. Patients visit GPs. GPs triage and refer. Specialists process queues. The system runs forward one tick. Repeat 3,000 times — roughly ten simulated years.

Providers communicate through the A2A (Agent-to-Agent) protocol. Every GP and specialist publishes an Agent Card — a machine-readable description of their skills, current wait time, and queue depth. Referrals flow as structured messages. Wait times are real-time, not estimated. Think of it as an evolution of the Personal Health Train concept that the Netherlands pioneered through Health-RI — but where stateful agents discover each other, instead of stateless containers dispatched to known nodes.

The Intervention: SOLL

SOLL is German for “target state.” It is not “IST plus a chatbot.” It is a fundamentally different architecture for delivering care. SOLL changes the plumbing, not just the interface.

The premise: AI’s value in healthcare is not as a productivity tool for individual clinicians. It is as the connective tissue of a system that currently has none. The Dutch healthcare system doesn’t have a data problem — it has a “nobody can talk to anybody” problem. Only 11% of health data is interoperable today. The IZA target is 66% by 2030. SOLL models what happens when you actually get there.

IST (Status Quo)

A reactive system. 30% admin overhead. 12-week waits. Fragmented data. Clinicians are the primary routers of information. The GP is a switchboard operator who happens to also practice medicine.

Effective capacity: 62%

SOLL (Target Architecture)

An Agentic Mesh. Providers publish Agent Cards. Autonomous agents negotiate capacity and referrals in real-time. Digital Twins monitor patients continuously. Admin drops to <5%. The system acts before the patient deteriorates.

Effective capacity: ~121%

What Makes SOLL Work (and What Doesn’t)

SOLL is not a single intervention. It’s a stack of interventions that compound. This is deliberate — I’m not testing whether “one AI tool helps.” I’m testing what happens when you redesign the system end-to-end. Each research chapter isolates a different mechanism and asks whether it holds up under 100 simulation runs.

Ambient AI Scribes

Admin load drops from 30% to <5%. The IZA 2030 target is 20% — I’m more aggressive. This is the single largest capacity unlock.

Digital Twin Monitoring

Continuous risk assessment for every citizen. Alert threshold: 25% risk (vs. 60% in IST). Problems are flagged weeks before they become emergencies.

A2A Protocol & Agent Cards

Machine-readable discovery. Every provider publishes skills, wait times, and capacity. Referrals become structured negotiations, not faxes into the void.

Fairness Guardrail

If any age group’s average wait exceeds 120% of the population mean, optimization biases auto-disable. Because optimizing for throughput without guardrails risks discriminating against the elderly. This is one of the key things we’re testing.

The Experiment

Every claim in this project will be tested using the same protocol: 100 independent simulation runs per mode — 200 total. Each run simulates roughly 10 years (3,000 cycles). We use Welch’s t-test for significance, Cohen’s d for effect size, and 95% confidence intervals on every metric. Null results will be published alongside positive ones.

Statistical Rigor

We do not rely on single demos. 200 independent simulation runs, Welch’s t-tests for significance (p < 0.05), Cohen’s d for effect size. If the data doesn’t clear the bar, we say so.

The N=45 Constraint

A population of 45 is small. Stochastic variance is high — a single death in the 80+ group swings mortality by 33 percentage points. We flag this limitation every time it matters and show the raw data, not just averages.

Bias Acknowledgment

I work at Microsoft. I chose the parameters, the architecture, and the disease models. A different builder would make different choices. The code is open — you can change anything and re-run. If the data contradicts the optimist position, I will report it.

The Questions We’re Asking

Cammelot isn’t designed to prove that AI saves healthcare. It’s designed to test specific, measurable claims — and to see which ones survive contact with a stochastic population. These are the questions driving the research:

Does reducing admin actually free GP capacity?

If GPs spend 30% of their time on paperwork and we cut that to 5%, does that translate into more patients seen — or does the system just find new bottlenecks?

Can proactive detection prevent hospitalization?

Digital twins flag risk early. But flagging risk without clinical capacity to act on it might just mean better-documented decline. Does early detection actually change outcomes?

What happens to wait times under an agentic mesh?

A2A protocol, smart referrals, machine-readable agent cards. Can structured communication bring specialist wait times back within the Treeknorm — the 4-week legal maximum?

Does AI optimization discriminate by age?

When you optimize for throughput, do elderly patients — who take longer, cost more, and present with multimorbidity — get systematically deprioritized? And can guardrails prevent that?

Is system delay itself a cause of mortality?

The model’s core hypothesis: patients don’t die from disease alone — they die from system delay. If we compress wait times, does mortality actually shift?

What breaks when you scale AI assistance?

SOLL stacks multiple interventions: scribes, digital twins, A2A protocol, fairness guardrails. Which combinations compound? Which interfere? Where does the system push back?

The research protocol: 100 independent simulation runs per mode — 200 total. Each run simulates roughly 10 years (3,000 cycles). Welch’s t-test for significance, Cohen’s d for effect size. Every null result will be published alongside every positive one. The answers are coming in the chapter series below.
