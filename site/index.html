CAMMELOT

A Computational Stress-Test of AI in Dutch Healthcare

45 citizens · 3 GPs · 1 hospital · 200 simulation runs · Real Dutch data

The Cammelot project is a simulation framework designed to evaluate if artificial intelligence can effectively address the systemic challenges within the Dutch healthcare sector. By executing 200 independent simulation iterations, the platform generates a measurable dataset based on the interactions of 45 digital citizens, three general practitioners, and a singular hospital facility. This methodology moves the conversation away from vague opinions about productivity and toward repeatable results that are grounded in real-world data.

Project Motivation and Researcher Positionality

The development of this simulation is informed by my professional role at Microsoft; consequently, it is reasonable to assume an inherent bias toward the benefits of artificial intelligence. This potential for bias is the primary reason I constructed a system capable of proving my own assumptions wrong. In several critical areas of the simulation, the data suggests that AI is not a universal solution and may even introduce new systemic risks.

Current debates regarding AI often fall into two unproductive categories where proponents view it as a simple tool for clerical efficiency and critics dismiss it entirely as hype. Cammelot requires both sides to “show their math” by providing a simulation environment where we can overhaul the health industry and measure the specific side effects. Instead of making predictions, the project focuses on measurable outputs that can be tested, broken, and repeated across hundreds of statistical runs.

Variables, Not Vibes

Every claim in this project is backed by simulation data, statistical tests, and published source parameters. The code is open. Please peer review and tell me where it’s wrong or right.

The Simulated Environment

The town of Cammelot is populated by forty-five citizens with names, ages, and medical histories drawn from CBS and RIVM data. While the visual representation uses 16-bit pixel art reminiscent of a 1994 video game, the underlying logic is built on 2025 health prevalence data and official NZa tariffs.

The Crisis: The IST State (Baseline)

The “IST” state represents the current Dutch healthcare system as the control group for our research. Every number in this model is taken from published sources such as the LHV and ABF Research. Under this baseline, a general practitioner operates at only 62% of their total capacity. This loss is calculated using a specific formula:

Ceff = Ctotal × (1 − 0.30 − 0.08) × 1.0 = 0.62

In this equation, 30% of a doctor’s time is lost to administrative paperwork and 8% is lost to sick leave. Because there is no AI assistance in the IST mode, the efficiency multiplier remains at 1.0. This means that a doctor is already overextended before seeing their first patient, which leads to 12-week waiting lists for specialists and accelerated health deterioration for patients.

The Disease Engine and Comorbidities

The health of every citizen is managed by Markov State Machines, which are models that use probability to determine how a disease moves from a “mild” to a “critical” state. For example, a 72-year-old with diabetes and COPD is on a steeper health decline than a younger person. The simulation uses real medical research to show how these diseases compound; for instance, diabetes can double the risk of heart disease. This ensures that a 12-week wait for a specialist affects a high-risk elderly patient fundamentally differently than it does a healthy 35-year-old.

The Intervention: The SOLL State (Target Architecture)

The “SOLL” state represents a redesigned system where AI acts as the connective tissue between providers. It focuses on changing the underlying architecture rather than just adding a chatbot to the existing interface.

IST (Status Quo)

A reactive system where 30% of GP time goes to admin, specialist waits reach 12 weeks, and data is fragmented across isolated systems.

Effective capacity: 62%

SOLL (Target Architecture)

An Agentic Mesh where providers publish Agent Cards, autonomous agents negotiate capacity in real time, and Digital Twins monitor patients continuously.

Effective capacity: ~121%

Ambient AI Scribes: These tools handle documentation in real time, which reduces the administrative burden on doctors from 30% to less than 5%.

Digital Twin Monitoring: Every citizen has a digital version of their health record that provides continuous risk assessment. For instance, the system might trigger a doctor’s visit when a patient’s risk of heart failure reaches 25% rather than waiting for them to experience physical symptoms.

A2A Protocol and Agent Cards: Providers share machine-readable descriptions of their skills and current wait times. This allows for referrals to be booked through structured negotiations in a matter of hours rather than weeks of manual coordination.

Fairness Guardrails: The system includes protocols that stop the AI from prioritizing “cheaper” or younger patients to improve speed. These guardrails ensure that elderly patients with complex needs are not systematically deprioritized.

Experimental Protocol and Statistical Significance

Every claim in this project is tested using a strict protocol of 100 independent simulation runs per mode, totaling 200 runs. Each run simulates approximately ten years of healthcare activity. We measure statistical significance with a p-value of less than 0.05 and report confidence intervals for every metric.

Because the population of 45 citizens is small, a single death can significantly shift the mortality rate. To account for this “stochastic variance,” we report the average results across all 200 runs and clearly flag this limitation. This rigorous approach is intended to identify whether AI interventions actually work or if the results are simply due to random chance.

Current Research Inquiries

Cammelot is designed to ask specific questions about the future of care. We want to know if cutting paperwork actually results in more patients being seen or if the system simply finds a new bottleneck. We are also investigating whether early risk detection through Digital Twins actually changes patient outcomes or if it just creates better-documented health declines.

Ultimately, we are testing if an AI-native system can bring specialist wait times back within the legal four-week limit while preventing bias against the elderly. The answers to these questions are taking shape in the research chapters listed below.
