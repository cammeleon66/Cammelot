Chapter 1

How the Simulation Works

“The goal is not to predict the future. It is to produce numbers you can argue about instead of opinions you can’t.”

Cammelot is a simulated Dutch town. It runs in your browser. It contains forty-five citizens with names, ages, and medical histories drawn from CBS and RIVM data. They walk around a 16-bit pixel map, get sick, visit GPs, get referred to specialists, wait in queues, and—if the system fails them—die.

Everything that happens in Cammelot is measurable. Every consultation has a cost from the NZa tariff tables. Every waiting time is checked against the Treeknorm, the Dutch legal norm for specialist care access. Every death is traceable to a specific bottleneck: how many weeks waited, which condition, which referral came too late.

It is deliberately small. Forty-five citizens, not five thousand. Three GPs, not twelve thousand. One hospital, not ninety. This is a design choice: small enough to trace every individual outcome, large enough to produce statistically meaningful differences across 200 runs.

The Town

The map is a 64×48 tile grid rendered on an HTML5 canvas. Buildings are placed at fixed positions: three GP practices distributed across the town, one regional hospital, and residential areas where citizens live. The aesthetic is deliberately retro—a 16-bit SNES-style RPG—because it forces attention away from visual fidelity and toward system behavior. You watch the town the way you watch an ant farm: not admiring the scenery, but tracking the patterns.

Citizens move between locations using simple pathfinding. When a citizen needs care, they walk to their assigned GP. If referred, they walk to the hospital. If the queue is full, they wait. You can see the queues build up visually—sprites clustering around buildings, speech bubbles expressing frustration or pain.

The Citizens

Each citizen is an agent with a persistent identity. They have a name, age, gender, occupation, personality traits, a family structure, and a GP assignment. Their health profile is generated from RIVM 2024 prevalence data: the probability of having type 2 diabetes, COPD, cardiovascular disease, osteoporosis, or dementia is age-stratified to match Dutch population statistics.

A citizen is not a row in a spreadsheet. They are a state machine with memory. They remember their GP visits, their referrals, their wait times. They experience disease progression through a Markov chain: healthy → mild → moderate → severe → critical. Each transition has a probability calibrated to RIVM data. Some citizens have comorbidities—COPD and cardiovascular disease together, for instance—which affect progression rates through documented relative risk multipliers.

The Providers

Cammelot has three GP practices and one hospital with three specialist departments. Each provider is also an agent—not just a queue. They have capacity limits, administrative burden, sick leave rates, and a cognitive loop that determines how they triage patients.

How Time Works

The simulation runs in discrete ticks. Each tick represents one step of the simulation clock. On the website, ticks fire every 500 milliseconds. In batch mode (for statistical runs), ticks fire every 200ms.

Every tick, each citizen’s state machine advances: disease may progress, HP may drain, they may decide to seek care. Every tick, each provider processes their queue: the next patient is seen, referrals are sent, capacity is recalculated.

A single simulation runs for approximately 200 ticks, representing roughly one year of healthcare activity. The key events that occur each tick:

Per-tick simulation loop
  1. Disease progression — Markov transitions checked for each citizen
  2. HP drain — citizens waiting beyond Treeknorm lose health points
  3. Care seeking — symptomatic citizens visit their GP
  4. Triage & referral — GPs refer to specialists via structured messages
  5. Queue processing — specialists see patients in order, respecting capacity
  6. Mortality check — citizens at HP ≤ 0 become ghost sprites

What We Measure

Every simulation run produces four categories of output. These are not arbitrary metrics—they correspond to the real indicators that the IZA (Integraal Zorgakkoord) uses to evaluate healthcare system performance.

Mortality: How many citizens die from preventable delays. Each death is traced to a specific cause chain: condition severity, weeks waited beyond Treeknorm, the referral that came too late. Citizens who die become ghost sprites—a visual reminder that system delays have consequences.

Wait times: Measured against the Treeknorm—the Dutch legal norm (4 weeks for diagnostics, 7–12 weeks for treatment). The simulation tracks how many citizens exceed these norms and by how much.

Costs: Every clinical interaction generates a cost from the NZa tariff tables. The simulation totals consultation costs, hospital days, and calculates the cost of prevented (or not prevented) hospitalizations. A prevented hospitalization saves an average of €5,845.

Fairness: Do older citizens get systematically worse outcomes? Do citizens with multiple conditions wait longer? The simulation tracks outcome variance across age groups and condition profiles to surface structural bias.

200 Runs

A single simulation run tells you a story. Two hundred runs tell you a pattern.

Because the simulation involves stochastic elements—disease progression probabilities, queue ordering, comorbidity interactions—individual runs vary. A citizen who dies in run 47 might survive in run 48 because the queue cleared one tick earlier. To distinguish signal from noise, every scenario is run 200 times.

The outputs are analyzed with standard statistical methods: means, standard deviations, confidence intervals. When comparing IST (current system) to SOLL (AI-augmented system), we use effect sizes and significance testing to determine whether observed differences are meaningful or just variance.

Why 200? With 45 citizens per run, individual variation is high. At N=200 runs, the standard error of the mean is small enough to detect effect sizes of 0.3 or larger at p<0.05. This is the minimum needed to make credible claims about system-level changes.

The results are presented in Chapter 2 (the current system’s baseline) and Chapter 3 (what changes when we add AI interventions). The raw data and simulation code are open source.
