Chapter 2

The Current System

“Before you can fix something, you have to prove it’s broken. With numbers.”

The Netherlands has one of Europe’s best-funded healthcare systems. Universal coverage. High patient satisfaction. Excellent acute care outcomes. By most international rankings, it’s a top-five system globally.

It’s still breaking.

The Dutch call it the zorginfarct — care infarction. Not a future scenario. Not a projection. It’s happening now: waiting lists that exceed legal norms, GP practices that close to new patients, specialists who can’t see referrals for months. The IZA (Integraal Zorgakkoord) projects a shortage of 301,000 healthcare workers by 2035 (ABF Research). That’s not a staffing gap. That’s a systemic failure.

The demographics are unforgiving. Twenty percent of the Dutch population is over 65. Among those 75 and older, 96% have at least one chronic condition (RIVM 2024). Forty-five percent have two or more. The system was designed for acute care in a younger population. The population aged. The system didn’t adapt.

This chapter explains the specific mechanisms by which the Dutch healthcare system is failing — and how Cammelot models each one.

Why the System Breaks

The zorginfarct is not a single failure. It’s three interlocking failure mechanisms, each making the others worse.

Administrative burden. A Dutch GP spends 30% of their working time on paperwork — electronic health records, referral letters, insurance coding, prescription management, quality reporting (LHV 2024). That’s not a technology problem. It’s a workflow architecture problem. The administrative burden is additive: every new regulation, every quality metric, every reporting requirement adds minutes per patient that never get removed. The cumulative effect is that a GP who could theoretically see 200 patients per week can effectively see about 124.

Waiting lists and Treeknorm violations. The Treeknorm is the Dutch legal norm for maximum acceptable wait times. Diagnostics: 4 weeks. Treatment: 7–12 weeks. These aren’t aspirational targets — they’re regulatory requirements. In practice: ophthalmology averages 12 weeks. Neurology and gynecology wait times have increased 50%+ since 2022. Among MRI and CT diagnostics, 28.9% exceed the 4-week norm (NZa 2024). When patients wait beyond the Treeknorm, their conditions deteriorate. What could have been treated in primary care becomes an emergency department visit. What could have been outpatient becomes inpatient. The system creates the complexity it can’t handle.

Data fragmentation. The Netherlands has roughly 12,000 GP practices, 90 hospital organisations, and 8 academic medical centres. Most data exchange between them still uses EDIFACT — a messaging standard from the 1980s originally designed for shipping container logistics. Only 11% of Dutch health data is interoperable between providers (IZA 2023). The national EHR is a virtual pointer system: it doesn’t store data, it points to it. Five providers treating the same patient maintain five independent records. The IZA has allocated €2.8 billion in transformation funding to reach 66% interoperability by 2030. Over 270 transformation plans were submitted before the postbus closed in July 2025.

How Cammelot Models This

Cammelot is a browser-based agent simulation that reproduces these failure mechanisms in miniature. The simulation creates a small Dutch town with the following structure:

  • 45 citizen agents with names, ages, and health conditions drawn from CBS/RIVM demographic distributions
  • 3 GP practices: Dr. de Jong (2,400 max patients), Dr. Bakker (2,000), Dr. Visser (1,800)
  • 1 regional hospital with 3 specialist departments: Cardiology (Dr. van den Berg, 12/week), Endocrinology (Dr. Mulder, 15/week), Pulmonology (Dr. Janssen, 10/week)

Each citizen starts with 100 health points (HP). Citizens develop conditions following RIVM prevalence rates, visit their assigned GP, receive referrals, and wait. The simulation runs in 500ms ticks, each representing roughly one day of simulated time.

The critical design choice: mortality in Cammelot is a consequence of system delay, not random chance. When a citizen waits beyond the Treeknorm threshold, their health deteriorates at a rate determined by their condition severity and the system’s administrative burden. HP reaches zero, the citizen dies, and their sprite turns grey. Every death in the simulation is traceable to a specific bottleneck.

In IST mode (current system): L_admin = 0.30 (30% administrative burden, LHV 2024), V_ziekte = 0.08 (8% sick leave / ziekteverzuim, CBS), E_ai = 1.0 (no AI assistance). The result: C_eff = 0.62. GPs operate at 62% of their theoretical capacity before seeing their first patient.

This isn’t an abstract number. It means that a GP practice with a norm of 2,400 patients has an effective capacity of roughly 1,488 patient-equivalent slots. The remaining 912 slots are consumed by administration, sick leave, and structural overhead. Every patient beyond 1,488 adds pressure that manifests as longer consultations, shorter attention spans, delayed referrals, and eventually — burnout.

Each condition progresses through a 5-state Markov chain: healthy → mild → moderate → severe → critical → deceased. Transition probabilities are evaluated per tick. The key mechanism: when a patient’s specialist wait time exceeds the Treeknorm threshold, probability mass shifts toward worse states. Waiting doesn’t just delay treatment — it actively accelerates disease progression.

Comorbidity multipliers model the clinical reality that conditions interact:

  • COPD → Cardiovascular Disease: relative risk = 2.5 (PMC)
  • Type 2 Diabetes → Cardiovascular Disease: relative risk = 2.0 (ESC Guidelines)
  • Hypertension → Cardiovascular Disease: relative risk = 1.8 (ESC Guidelines)

A patient with both COPD and Type 2 Diabetes faces a compounded cardiovascular risk that the system’s waiting lists make materially worse.

Two thresholds trigger state changes: HP ≤ 20 puts the citizen in a distressed state with urgent referral priority. HP ≤ 0 means death — the citizen’s sprite turns grey (a “ghost”). The (1 + adminBurden) term is deliberate: administrative overhead doesn’t just reduce capacity, it actively accelerates patient deterioration by delaying every step of the care chain.

Modeling Decisions and Assumptions

⚠️ Limitations and honest caveats

Cammelot is a simplified model. These are the assumptions and their implications:

  • Population of 45 is small. Stochastic variance is high. Individual runs can produce outlier results. This is why we report averages across 200 runs, not individual simulations.
  • Disease progression is simplified. The 5-state Markov chain (healthy → mild → moderate → severe → critical) is not clinical reality. Real disease trajectories involve remission, acute exacerbations, treatment response variation, and comorbidity interactions that are far more complex than our transition matrices capture.
  • GP capacity model assumes uniform distribution of patient complexity. In practice, some consultations are 5-minute prescription renewals; others are 45-minute multimorbidity assessments. Our model treats them equivalently.
  • Mortality from system delay is modeled, not observed. No randomised controlled trial exists for “what happens if you make people wait.” Our HP drain mechanism is a modeling construct, not a clinical finding. The direction is supported by evidence (delayed treatment worsens outcomes), but the specific drain rates are calibrated parameters, not measured values.
  • All parameters derive from published Dutch data sources (CBS, RIVM, NZa, IZA, LHV), but the model is a simplification. We do not claim predictive accuracy for individual patients. We claim that the system dynamics — bottleneck formation, queue growth, capacity erosion — reproduce the qualitative patterns observed in the real Dutch healthcare system.
  • Natural mortality (age-based, from CBS life tables) is tracked separately from system-failure mortality. The simulation distinguishes between “died because they were 92” and “died because they waited 18 weeks for a cardiologist.”

The structural argument The current system doesn’t fail because people aren’t trying. It fails because the architecture — administrative, clinical, and digital — was designed for a younger, healthier population with fewer chronic conditions. The demographics changed. The architecture didn’t.
