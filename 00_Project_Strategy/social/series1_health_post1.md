## Post

There's a lot of skepticism about AI right now. I get it.

Hype cycles, hallucinations, job anxiety, ethics debates. The doubts are legitimate.

But there's a question I don't hear enough:

**What is the cost of NOT changing?**

---

In the Netherlands, the math is already brutal:
- **301,000 healthcare workers short by 2035** — that's not a projection, that's CBS
- GPs burning **30% of their working hours on administration**, not patients
- Specialist waiting lists breaching legal norms across the board
- 96% of people over 75 living with at least one chronic condition

This isn't a future problem. It's arriving now.

The debate about AI tends to happen in the abstract — productivity tools, chatbots, ROI on software licenses. Meanwhile, a system that 17 million people depend on is approaching structural failure.

**You don't get to be a skeptic without an alternative.**

---

So I built one. Or rather — I built the question.

**Cammelot** is a simulation of a 5,000-person Dutch town. Citizens age, develop conditions drawn from real RIVM statistics, try to access care, wait in queues, get referred, deteriorate. Some don't make it.

The first version (IST) models the status quo. GPs overloaded. Admin burden crippling capacity. Waiting lists that cost lives — literally. Citizens who run out of HP before they reach a specialist become ghosts.

The second version (SOLL) rebuilds from the bottom up: structured data shared across providers, AI-native triage, ambient scribes that eliminate the admin tax. Every agent with access to a full digital twin.

Then I let applied research questions loose on both worlds:
- Does agentic AI worsen or reduce inequality of care across age groups?
- What's the actual € cost of the 30% admin burden?
- Can proactive digital twin triage reduce ER pressure by 30%+?

---

This is the start of a research series. Not marketing. Not hype. Actual model outputs, sourced from CBS, RIVM, NZa, and IZA — with the simulation code on GitHub.

The healthcare sector **has** to change. The only real question is whether we're thoughtful about how.

I'm looking for humans in the loop — healthcare professionals, researchers, policy people, and yes, skeptics especially. **What would you test?**
